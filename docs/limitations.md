# Limitations and Threats to Validity

**Community Technology Survey — BTS 2026**

This document enumerates known limitations and threats to the validity of the findings. Transparency about limitations is a core requirement of rigorous research.

---

## 1. Sampling Limitations

**Convenience/purposive sampling.** Respondents were recruited through BTS networks, social media, and direct outreach. This is not a random or representative sample of the Newfoundland & Labrador population. Findings describe the views of people who chose to participate, not the general public.

**Self-selection bias.** People who respond to a survey about community technology are likely to have stronger-than-average interest in the topic. Satisfaction scores and support for the co-op model may be inflated compared to the broader population.

**Digital access bias.** The survey was online-only in English. People without internet access, with low digital literacy, or who primarily communicate in French, Inuktitut, Innu-aimun, or other languages are under-represented.

**Small segment sizes.** Most non-public-respondent segments (co-ops, unions, non-profits, municipalities, activists) have n = 1–6. Statistical generalisations within these groups are not possible. Findings for small segments are exploratory and hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.

---

## 2. Instrument Limitations

**No pilot testing.** The survey was not formally piloted before deployment. Ambiguous questions, poor response option coverage, or cognitive burden issues were not empirically detected before data collection.

**Branching complexity.** The branching survey design means different respondents saw different questions. Comparing findings across sections requires careful attention to denominators.

**Social desirability bias.** Questions about community ownership, governance, and trust are normatively framed in the direction of community co-operation. Respondents may have reported more positive attitudes than they hold privately, particularly in a survey distributed by the organisation proposing the co-op.

**Response option coverage.** Some questions may not have included all relevant response options, leading respondents to select "Other" or leave questions blank. "Other" responses and open-text fields are important supplementary data.

---

## 3. Analytical Limitations

**Cross-sectional design.** The survey captures a single point in time. Attitudes and conditions may change as the platform co-op progresses (or does not).

**AI-assisted qualitative coding.** LLM-generated codes and themes are not equivalent to human researcher coding. LLMs may miss cultural nuance, misinterpret ambiguous language, or produce plausible-sounding but inaccurate codes. Human analyst review of themes is essential; this should be documented in the analyst notes section of `docs/codebook.md`.

**Intercoder reliability limitation.** The second coding pass for IRR uses the same LLM model with identical prompts — not an independent human coder. True human-LLM or human-human intercoder reliability was not assessed due to resource constraints. This is a methodological limitation that future studies should address.

**Ordinal vs. interval treatment.** The decision to treat 5-point Likert items as interval-level (for mean reporting) is a convention, not a mathematically established fact. Readers who prefer strictly ordinal analysis should refer to the median and IQR statistics reported alongside means.

---

## 4. Positionality and Conflict of Interest

Better Together Solutions, the organisation that administered and analysed this survey, is the same organisation that is proposing to build the community platform co-operative. This creates a potential conflict of interest at every stage:
- Instrument design may reflect desired findings
- Recruitment channels may over-recruit supporters
- Interpretation of ambiguous findings may favour optimistic readings

**Mitigations applied:**
- Analysis plan was committed to version control before analysis (pre-registration equivalent)
- All code, data, and prompts are published for independent review
- Findings that do not support the proposal are reported alongside those that do
- This limitations section explicitly names the conflict

We encourage independent researchers to re-analyse the published dataset and challenge our interpretations.

---

## 5. Generalisability

These findings should not be generalised beyond the study population. They describe the views of 112 people who chose to respond to this survey in April 2026. They are most appropriately used as:
- Baseline data for future, more rigorous studies
- Community dialogue starters
- Evidence of demand (not proof of market size)
- Hypothesis generation for further research
